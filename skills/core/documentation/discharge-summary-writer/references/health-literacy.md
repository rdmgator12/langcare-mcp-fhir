# Health Literacy and Patient Instruction Guidelines

## Health Literacy Overview

### The Problem

- **36% of US adults** have basic or below-basic health literacy (NAAL, National Assessment of Adult Literacy)
- Average American reads at an **8th grade level**; many healthcare materials are written at 10th-12th grade level
- Low health literacy is independently associated with: increased hospitalization, higher readmission rates, worse medication adherence, increased mortality, higher healthcare costs
- Populations at higher risk: elderly, non-native English speakers, low income, low educational attainment

### Target Readability for Patient Instructions

| Measure | Target | How to Assess |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level | 5th-6th grade | Most word processors calculate this |
| Flesch Reading Ease | > 60 (ideally > 70) | Higher score = easier to read |
| SMOG Index | <= 6th grade | Count polysyllabic words |
| Gunning Fog Index | <= 8 | Measures sentence and word complexity |

### CMS Requirements for Patient Materials

- Written at or below **6th grade reading level** for Medicare beneficiaries
- Available in languages spoken by **> 5%** of the service area population
- Must be culturally appropriate
- Must be accessible (large print, audio, etc. for patients with disabilities)

## Plain Language Principles

### Core Rules

1. **Use common words**: "take" not "administer", "swelling" not "edema", "blood thinner" not "anticoagulant"
2. **Short sentences**: Aim for 10-15 words per sentence
3. **Active voice**: "Take your medicine at 8am" not "Your medicine should be taken at 8am"
4. **One idea per sentence**: Break complex instructions into steps
5. **Avoid jargon**: If a medical term must be used, define it immediately
6. **Use "you"**: Direct address makes instructions personal and clear
7. **Be specific**: "Take 2 pills in the morning" not "Take as directed"

### Medication Instruction Simplification

| Complex Instruction | Plain Language |
|--------------------|---------------|
| "Take 1 tablet PO BID with meals" | "Take 1 pill by mouth 2 times a day with breakfast and dinner" |
| "Administer 10 units SQ QHS" | "Give yourself 10 units as a shot under the skin at bedtime" |
| "Apply topically TID to affected area" | "Put the cream on the sore spot 3 times a day" |
| "Take PRN Q4-6H for pain" | "Take when you have pain. Wait at least 4 to 6 hours between doses." |
| "Titrate as tolerated" | "Your doctor will slowly increase the dose" |
| "Taper: reduce by 5mg q3d" | "Take less and less of this medicine: lower your dose by 1 pill every 3 days" |
| "Hold if SBP < 100" | "Do NOT take this pill if your blood pressure (top number) is below 100" |
| "Take on empty stomach" | "Take this pill 1 hour before eating or 2 hours after eating" |

## Health Literacy Assessment

### Screening Tools

#### Single-Item Literacy Screener (SILS)
"How often do you need to have someone help you when you read instructions, pamphlets, or other written material from your doctor or pharmacy?"

| Response | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Never | Standard materials likely adequate |
| Rarely | Standard materials likely adequate |
| Sometimes | Use simplified materials, confirm understanding |
| Often | Use simplified materials, visual aids, teach-back |
| Always | Use verbal instruction, pictorial aids, teach-back, involve caregiver |

#### Newest Vital Sign (NVS)
- 6-question tool using a nutrition label
- Takes 3 minutes to administer
- Score 0-1: High likelihood of limited literacy
- Score 2-3: Possible limited literacy
- Score 4-6: Adequate literacy

#### REALM (Rapid Estimate of Adult Literacy in Medicine)
- 66 medical words patient reads aloud
- Scored by pronunciation accuracy
- Correlates to grade level

### Assessing Understanding Without Formal Tools

Observe for:
- Asks no questions (may indicate confusion, not comprehension)
- Cannot name medications or their purposes
- Misses appointments frequently
- Forms completed incorrectly or incompletely
- Brings someone to "help explain"
- States "I'll read it when I get home"
- Avoids reading materials in the office

## Teach-Back Method

### What Is Teach-Back?

A communication technique where the provider asks the patient to explain, in their own words, what they have been told. The responsibility for clear communication falls on the provider, not the patient.

### How to Use Teach-Back

#### Step 1: Explain
Provide the information in plain language, one concept at a time.

#### Step 2: Assess
Ask the patient to explain it back. Use non-shaming language:

**Effective prompts:**
- "I want to make sure I explained this clearly. Can you tell me in your own words what you will do when you get home?"
- "I've given you a lot of information. To make sure I was clear, can you tell me the 3 most important things about your new medicine?"
- "Many patients find this confusing. Can you explain to me how you will take your medicine?"

**Avoid:**
- "Do you understand?" (Patient will say yes regardless)
- "Do you have any questions?" (Most patients will say no)
- "Repeat what I just said" (Feels like a test)

#### Step 3: Re-teach if Needed
If the patient cannot accurately explain:
- Do NOT repeat the same explanation
- Rephrase using different words
- Use visual aids, diagrams, or demonstrations
- Break information into smaller chunks
- Re-assess with teach-back

#### Step 4: Document
Document in the discharge summary:
- "Patient education provided regarding [topic]."
- "Teach-back method used. Patient able to verbalize [key points]."
- Or: "Patient unable to demonstrate understanding; caregiver [name] instructed and verbalized understanding."

### Teach-Back for Common Discharge Scenarios

#### New Medication
"I want to make sure I explained your new blood thinner clearly. Can you tell me:
1. What is the name of your new medicine?
2. How often will you take it?
3. What should you watch for that would make you call us?"

#### Warning Signs
"Before you go home, I want to make sure you know when to come back. Can you tell me what problems would make you call 911 or come to the ER?"

#### Follow-Up Plan
"You have some important appointments coming up. Can you tell me:
1. Who you are seeing next?
2. When that appointment is?
3. What tests need to be done before that visit?"

#### Wound Care
"You will need to take care of your incision at home. Can you show me how you would change the bandage? And tell me what would worry you about the wound?"

## Document Formatting for Low Literacy

### Layout Principles

1. **White space**: Leave generous margins and space between sections
2. **Headers**: Use clear, descriptive headers for each section
3. **Lists**: Use bulleted or numbered lists instead of paragraphs
4. **Bold for emphasis**: Highlight key actions (take, call, go to ER)
5. **Font**: Sans-serif (Arial, Calibri), minimum 12-point, 14-point preferred for elderly
6. **No ALL CAPS**: Harder to read than mixed case (except for single-word warnings)
7. **Images**: Use simple illustrations when possible (pill images, body diagrams)

### Instruction Structure

For each instruction set:

```
WHAT TO DO:
  [Action in 1-2 simple sentences]

WHEN TO DO IT:
  [Specific timing: morning, with dinner, before bed]

HOW TO DO IT:
  1. [Step 1]
  2. [Step 2]
  3. [Step 3]

WHEN TO GET HELP:
  Call your doctor at [phone number] if:
  - [Warning sign 1]
  - [Warning sign 2]

  Go to the Emergency Room or call 911 if:
  - [Emergency sign 1]
  - [Emergency sign 2]
```

### Multilingual Considerations

- Identify patient's preferred language from `Patient.communication` where `preferred = true`
- If patient's preferred language is not English, instructions should be provided in that language
- Use professional medical interpreters for verbal instruction (not family members for medical translation)
- Document: "Instructions provided in [language] via [certified interpreter / translated document]"
- Common languages requiring translated materials: Spanish, Chinese (Mandarin/Cantonese), Vietnamese, Korean, Tagalog, Russian, Arabic, Haitian Creole, French, Portuguese

### Cultural Considerations

- Assess cultural beliefs about illness and treatment
- Identify decision-making patterns (individual vs family-centered)
- Dietary restrictions (religious, cultural) affecting nutritional instructions
- Attitudes toward medication (some cultures prefer natural remedies)
- Attitudes toward mental health and substance use
- Gender preferences for healthcare providers
- Traditional healing practices that may interact with prescribed treatment
- Document cultural considerations that affect the discharge plan

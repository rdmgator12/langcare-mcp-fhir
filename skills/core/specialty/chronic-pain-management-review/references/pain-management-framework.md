# Pain Management Framework Reference

## WHO Analgesic Ladder

Originally developed for cancer pain; adapted for chronic non-cancer pain. Stepped approach to analgesic selection based on pain severity.

### Three-Step Ladder

| Step | Pain Severity | Agents | Notes |
|------|-------------|--------|-------|
| Step 1 | Mild (1-3/10) | Non-opioid analgesics: acetaminophen, NSAIDs | Add adjuvants as appropriate |
| Step 2 | Moderate (4-6/10) | Weak opioids: tramadol, codeine (+ non-opioids) | Or low-dose strong opioids. Add adjuvants. |
| Step 3 | Severe (7-10/10) | Strong opioids: morphine, oxycodone, hydromorphone, fentanyl (+ non-opioids) | Add adjuvants. Lowest effective dose. |

### Modern Adaptations

The WHO ladder is now considered a starting framework, not a rigid protocol. Current best practice emphasizes:
- Multimodal analgesia at every step (not just pharmacologic)
- Interventional procedures can be introduced at any step
- Biopsychosocial model: address psychological and social contributors
- Functional goals over pain score targets
- Step-down approach: consider opioid tapering when pain is chronic and stable

## Multimodal Pain Management Framework

### Core Principle

Combine multiple modalities targeting different pain pathways to achieve better analgesia with fewer side effects from any single agent. No single modality should be used in isolation for chronic pain.

### Modality Categories

| Category | Examples | Evidence Level |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Non-opioid pharmacotherapy | NSAIDs, acetaminophen, topicals | Strong for nociceptive pain |
| Adjuvant analgesics | Gabapentinoids, SNRIs, TCAs, muscle relaxants | Strong for neuropathic pain |
| Opioid therapy | When indicated, lowest effective dose | Moderate; risks increase with duration |
| Interventional procedures | Epidural injections, nerve blocks, radiofrequency ablation, spinal cord stimulation | Moderate to strong depending on condition |
| Physical therapy / Rehabilitation | Exercise therapy, manual therapy, aquatic therapy | Strong for musculoskeletal pain |
| Behavioral / Psychological | CBT for pain, acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT), biofeedback, mindfulness-based stress reduction | Strong |
| Complementary / Integrative | Acupuncture, massage, yoga, tai chi | Moderate |
| Self-management | Patient education, activity pacing, sleep hygiene, weight management | Supportive |

### Recommended Multimodal Combinations by Pain Type

**Chronic low back pain:**
1. Exercise therapy + NSAIDs (first-line)
2. Add: duloxetine or gabapentin if neuropathic component
3. Consider: CBT, spinal manipulation, acupuncture
4. Interventional: epidural steroid injections, radiofrequency ablation for facet-mediated pain
5. Opioids: only if other modalities insufficient, short-term trial with clear goals

**Fibromyalgia:**
1. Exercise (aerobic + resistance) is the strongest evidence intervention
2. Duloxetine or pregabalin (FDA-approved for fibromyalgia)
3. CBT for pain
4. Sleep optimization (CBT-I, trazodone, or low-dose amitriptyline)
5. Opioids: NOT recommended (no evidence of efficacy, high risk of harm)

**Neuropathic pain:**
1. Gabapentin or pregabalin (first-line)
2. Duloxetine or venlafaxine (first-line, especially with comorbid depression)
3. TCA (amitriptyline, nortriptyline) -- effective but more side effects
4. Topical lidocaine (localized neuropathic pain)
5. Capsaicin 8% patch (localized)
6. Tramadol or opioids: second/third-line only

**Osteoarthritis:**
1. Exercise + weight management (most important)
2. Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel) -- first-line pharmacotherapy for knee/hand OA
3. Oral NSAIDs (with GI protection if needed)
4. Duloxetine (FDA-approved for chronic musculoskeletal pain)
5. Intra-articular corticosteroid injections (short-term benefit)
6. Acetaminophen (limited efficacy but safe for many patients)
7. Opioids: last resort, short-term only

## Neuropathic vs Nociceptive Pain Differentiation

### Nociceptive Pain

| Feature | Description |
|---------|------------|
| Mechanism | Tissue damage activating nociceptors |
| Quality | Aching, throbbing, sharp, dull, pressure |
| Location | Well-localized, correlates with injury/pathology |
| Examples | Osteoarthritis, fracture, post-surgical, inflammatory |
| Response to NSAIDs | Good |
| Response to opioids | Good |
| First-line Rx | NSAIDs, acetaminophen, physical therapy |

### Neuropathic Pain

| Feature | Description |
|---------|------------|
| Mechanism | Nerve damage or dysfunction |
| Quality | Burning, shooting, electric, tingling, numbness, pins-and-needles |
| Location | Follows nerve distribution (dermatome/peripheral nerve territory) |
| Examples | Diabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, radiculopathy, CRPS, phantom limb |
| Response to NSAIDs | Poor |
| Response to opioids | Partial |
| First-line Rx | Gabapentinoids, SNRIs, TCAs |

### Nociplastic Pain

| Feature | Description |
|---------|------------|
| Mechanism | Altered nociceptive processing (central sensitization) without clear tissue or nerve damage |
| Quality | Widespread, diffuse, often migratory, hypersensitivity to stimuli |
| Location | Widespread, not following anatomic distribution |
| Examples | Fibromyalgia, chronic widespread pain, irritable bowel syndrome, chronic pelvic pain |
| Response to NSAIDs | Poor |
| Response to opioids | Poor (generally harmful) |
| First-line Rx | Exercise, duloxetine, pregabalin, CBT |

### Mixed Pain

Most chronic pain conditions have components of multiple pain types. Treat all identified mechanisms simultaneously:
- Low back pain with radiculopathy: nociceptive (disc/facet) + neuropathic (nerve root compression)
- Post-surgical chronic pain: nociceptive (tissue) + neuropathic (nerve damage) + possible nociplastic (central sensitization)

## PEG Scale (Pain, Enjoyment, General Activity)

### Purpose

Brief 3-item functional pain assessment. Recommended by the CDC for monitoring chronic pain treatment outcomes. Captures both pain intensity and functional impact.

### Items

| # | Question | Scale |
|---|----------|-------|
| 1 | What number best describes your pain on average in the past week? | 0 (no pain) to 10 (pain as bad as you can imagine) |
| 2 | What number best describes how, during the past week, pain has interfered with your enjoyment of life? | 0 (does not interfere) to 10 (completely interferes) |
| 3 | What number best describes how, during the past week, pain has interfered with your general activity? | 0 (does not interfere) to 10 (completely interferes) |

### Scoring

- Range: 0-30 (sum of 3 items) or 0-10 (average of 3 items)
- Average PEG score is typically reported
- Clinically meaningful improvement: >= 30% reduction in PEG score
- LOINC: 77565-0

### Interpretation

| Average PEG Score | Interpretation |
|------------------|---------------|
| 0-3 | Mild pain with minimal functional impact |
| 4-6 | Moderate pain with moderate functional impairment |
| 7-10 | Severe pain with significant functional impairment |

### Clinical Use

- Administer at each pain management visit
- Track trends over time
- If pain scores decreasing but function not improving: reassess treatment approach
- If function improving despite persistent pain scores: treatment may still be beneficial
- Functional improvement is the primary treatment goal, not pain elimination

## Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) Components

### Pain Severity (4 items, each 0-10)

1. Pain at its worst in the last 24 hours
2. Pain at its least in the last 24 hours
3. Pain on average
4. Pain right now

Severity score = mean of 4 items

### Pain Interference (7 items, each 0-10)

How pain has interfered with:
1. General activity
2. Mood
3. Walking ability
4. Normal work (both outside the home and housework)
5. Relations with other people
6. Sleep
7. Enjoyment of life

Interference score = mean of 7 items

### Clinical Significance

- BPI Severity >= 4: moderate pain
- BPI Interference >= 4: moderate functional impairment
- A 2-point change on any BPI scale is clinically meaningful

## Non-Pharmacologic Evidence-Based Interventions

### Strong Evidence (Recommended by ACP, AAFP, and/or CDC)

| Intervention | Conditions | Evidence |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Exercise therapy | Low back pain, OA, fibromyalgia, neck pain | Strongest non-pharmacologic evidence. Aerobic + resistance. |
| CBT for chronic pain | All chronic pain conditions | Reduces pain, disability, and catastrophizing. Durable effects. |
| Physical therapy | Musculoskeletal pain, post-surgical, radiculopathy | Manual therapy, therapeutic exercise, modalities |
| Spinal manipulation | Acute and chronic low back pain | Short-term benefit, comparable to other first-line treatments |
| Yoga | Chronic low back pain, fibromyalgia | Multiple RCTs showing benefit. Mind-body integration. |
| Tai chi | OA, fibromyalgia, chronic low back pain | Moderate evidence for pain and function improvement |
| Acupuncture | Chronic pain (low back, OA, headache, fibromyalgia) | Moderate evidence; ACP conditional recommendation for low back pain |
| Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) | Chronic pain, fibromyalgia | Reduces pain intensity and improves coping. 8-week structured program. |

### Moderate Evidence

| Intervention | Conditions | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Massage therapy | Musculoskeletal pain, low back pain | Short-term benefit. Best combined with exercise. |
| Heat/cold therapy | Acute musculoskeletal pain, low back pain | Self-management. Safe adjunct. |
| TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation) | Various pain conditions | Mixed evidence. May help some patients as adjunct. |
| Progressive muscle relaxation | Chronic pain with tension component | Free, teachable, can be done at home |
| Biofeedback | Headache, musculoskeletal pain | Teaches physiological self-regulation |
| Aquatic therapy | OA, fibromyalgia, chronic pain with mobility limitation | Reduced joint stress. Warm water adds pain relief. |

### Patient Education Topics for Self-Management

- Pain neuroscience education (understanding pain does not equal damage)
- Activity pacing (avoid boom-bust cycle)
- Sleep hygiene (poor sleep amplifies pain)
- Stress management techniques
- Goal-setting (functional goals, not pain elimination)
- Weight management (if applicable -- reduces mechanical load)
- Ergonomic modifications (workplace, home)
- Flare management plan (what to do when pain worsens)

## Nephrotoxic Drug List

Medications that can cause or worsen kidney injury. Avoid or use with extreme caution in CKD.

### Direct Nephrotoxins

| Drug/Class | Mechanism | Monitoring | Risk Mitigation |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, ketorolac) | Afferent arteriole vasoconstriction; reduced renal perfusion | Cr before and 1 week after starting | Avoid entirely in CKD G3-G5. Short courses only if absolutely necessary in G1-G2. |
| Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin) | Direct tubular toxicity | Daily Cr, trough levels, consider once-daily dosing | Use only when no alternative. Extended-interval dosing. Limit to 5-7 days. |
| Amphotericin B (conventional) | Tubular toxicity, renal vasoconstriction | Daily Cr, K+, Mg | Use liposomal formulation (less nephrotoxic). Pre-hydrate with NS. |
| Vancomycin | Tubular toxicity (especially with high trough levels) | AUC-guided dosing; Cr q24-48h | Target AUC/MIC 400-600; avoid troughs > 20 ug/mL. |
| IV contrast (iodinated) | Tubular toxicity, vasoconstriction | Cr before and 48-72h after | Pre-hydrate (NS 1 mL/kg/hr x 6-12h before and after). Use lowest volume of iso-osmolar contrast. Hold metformin. Avoid if eGFR < 30 unless benefit >> risk. |
| Cisplatin | Tubular toxicity | Cr, Mg, K+ before each cycle | Aggressive pre- and post-hydration. Amifostine consideration. |
| Methotrexate (high-dose) | Crystal nephropathy | Cr, MTX levels, urine pH | Aggressive hydration + alkalinization. Leucovorin rescue. Glucarpidase if levels remain high. |
| Lithium | Chronic tubulointerstitial nephritis, nephrogenic DI | Cr, eGFR q3-6 months; lithium levels | Maintain lowest effective level. Adequate hydration. |
| Tenofovir (TDF) | Proximal tubular toxicity (Fanconi syndrome) | Cr, phosphorus, urinalysis q3-6 months | Prefer TAF formulation (less nephrotoxic). Dose-adjust TDF by CrCl. |
| Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus) | Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction, chronic fibrosis | Drug levels, Cr, K+, Mg | Therapeutic drug monitoring. Target appropriate trough levels. |

### Indirect Nephrotoxins / Risk Factors

| Drug/Situation | Mechanism | Notes |
|---------------|-----------|-------|
| ACEi / ARB | Efferent arteriolar dilation; reduce GFR. Up to 30% Cr rise acceptable after initiation. | NOT nephrotoxic per se; nephroprotective long-term. Do NOT discontinue for mild Cr rise. Discontinue if Cr rises > 30% or K+ > 5.5. |
| Diuretics (excessive) | Prerenal AKI from volume depletion | Monitor volume status, Cr, electrolytes. Adjust dose to euvolemia. |
| PPI (long-term) | Acute interstitial nephritis; association with CKD | Consider step-down or discontinuation if on long-term. |
| Phosphate-based bowel preps (sodium phosphate) | Acute phosphate nephropathy | Avoid entirely in CKD. Use PEG-based preps. |
| IV immunoglobulin (IVIG, sucrose-containing) | Osmotic nephrosis | Use non-sucrose formulations. Slow infusion rate. Pre-hydrate. |
| Gadolinium (MRI contrast) | Nephrogenic systemic fibrosis (NSF) risk in eGFR < 30 | Use Group II agents (gadobutrol, gadoterate meglumine, gadobenate dimeglumine) which have lowest NSF risk. Avoid Group I agents. |

## Combination Risk: "Triple Whammy"

The combination of ACEi/ARB + diuretic + NSAID carries the highest risk of AKI. Avoid this combination, especially in CKD, elderly, and dehydrated patients.

Risk calculation:
- ACEi/ARB alone: minimal AKI risk (nephroprotective)
- ACEi/ARB + diuretic: moderate AKI risk (2x baseline)
- ACEi/ARB + diuretic + NSAID: high AKI risk (31x baseline per BMJ study)

# Electrolyte Management in CKD

Targets, monitoring intervals, and management strategies for electrolyte abnormalities by CKD stage. Includes dialysis initiation criteria.

## Potassium Management

### Targets by CKD Stage

| CKD Stage | Target Range | Monitoring Frequency | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|-------|
| G1-G2 | 3.5-5.0 mEq/L | Annually or with BMP | Standard range |
| G3a | 3.5-5.0 mEq/L | Every 6-12 months | Monitor if on ACEi/ARB/MRA |
| G3b | 3.5-5.0 mEq/L | Every 3-6 months | Hyperkalemia risk increases |
| G4 | 3.5-5.5 mEq/L | Every 1-3 months | Accept slightly higher upper limit |
| G5 / Dialysis | 3.5-5.5 mEq/L | Weekly to monthly | Pre-dialysis target |

### Hyperkalemia Management Algorithm

**K+ 5.1-5.5 mEq/L (mild):**
1. Verify specimen (no hemolysis)
2. Dietary counseling: limit high-K+ foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes, tomatoes, avocado, spinach)
3. Review medications: reduce/hold potassium supplements, potassium-sparing diuretics, TMP-SMX
4. Ensure adequate hydration and urine output
5. Recheck in 1-2 weeks

**K+ 5.5-6.0 mEq/L (moderate):**
1. All mild measures above
2. Consider reducing ACEi/ARB dose (do NOT automatically stop -- weigh proteinuria benefit)
3. Add loop diuretic if volume overloaded (furosemide 20-40mg)
4. Consider potassium binder: sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (Lokelma) 10g TID for 48h then 5-10g daily, or patiromer (Veltassa) 8.4g daily
5. Recheck in 48-72 hours

**K+ 6.0-6.5 mEq/L (severe):**
1. Obtain ECG immediately
2. If ECG changes (peaked T waves, widened QRS, loss of P waves): treat as emergency
3. Hold all potassium-elevating medications (ACEi/ARB, spironolactone, potassium supplements)
4. Potassium binder
5. Consider sodium bicarbonate if acidotic (shifts K+ intracellularly)
6. Recheck in 24 hours
7. Reassess ACEi/ARB when K+ < 5.5 (may restart at lower dose with binder)

**K+ > 6.5 mEq/L (emergency):**
1. ECG stat and continuous cardiac monitoring
2. Calcium gluconate 10% -- 10mL IV over 2-3 min (membrane stabilization, NOT K+ lowering; onset: 1-3 min, duration: 30-60 min)
3. Regular insulin 10 units IV + Dextrose 50% 25g IV (shifts K+ intracellularly; onset: 15-30 min, duration: 4-6h)
4. Albuterol nebulizer 10-20mg (additional shift; onset: 15-30 min)
5. Sodium bicarbonate 50-100 mEq IV if acidotic
6. Potassium binder (for GI elimination)
7. Loop diuretic IV (furosemide 40-80mg) if urine output present
8. Emergency hemodialysis if refractory or anuric

### Hypokalemia in CKD (less common but possible with diuretic use)

**K+ 3.0-3.5 mEq/L:**
- Oral KCl 20-40 mEq daily in divided doses
- Increase dietary potassium
- Check magnesium (correct if < 2.0 mg/dL -- refractory hypokalemia if Mg low)
- Reduce diuretic dose if possible

**K+ < 3.0 mEq/L:**
- IV KCl: 10-20 mEq/hr via peripheral (max 40 mEq/hr via central line)
- ECG monitoring
- Check and correct magnesium
- Hold diuretics

## Phosphorus Management

### Targets by CKD Stage

| CKD Stage | Target | Monitoring | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-----------|-------|
| G1-G3a | 2.5-4.5 mg/dL | Annually | Phosphorus usually normal until G3b+ |
| G3b | 2.5-4.5 mg/dL | Every 6-12 months | Beginning of phosphorus retention |
| G4 | 2.5-4.5 mg/dL | Every 3-6 months | Active management often needed |
| G5 / Dialysis | 3.5-5.5 mg/dL | Monthly | KDIGO suggests maintaining within "normal range" rather than strict target |

### Hyperphosphatemia Management

**Step 1: Dietary phosphorus restriction**
- Limit to 800-1000 mg/day
- Avoid processed foods (phosphate additives: look for "phos" in ingredient lists -- sodium phosphate, phosphoric acid, pyrophosphate)
- Prefer plant-based over animal-based phosphorus sources (lower bioavailability)
- Education: hidden phosphorus in processed meats, colas, fast food, cheese

**Step 2: Phosphate binders (take with meals)**

| Binder | Dose | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Calcium acetate (PhosLo) | 667mg (1-2 tabs) with each meal | Inexpensive, effective | Hypercalcemia risk; limit total Ca to < 1500mg/day |
| Calcium carbonate (Tums) | 500-1000mg with each meal | Inexpensive, OTC | Hypercalcemia risk; less effective than acetate |
| Sevelamer (Renagel/Renvela) | 800-1600mg with each meal | No calcium load; may lower LDL | GI side effects (constipation, nausea), pill burden |
| Lanthanum (Fosrenol) | 500-1000mg chewed with each meal | Effective, lower pill burden | GI side effects, accumulation concern |
| Sucroferric oxyhydroxide (Velphoro) | 500mg chewed with each meal | Low pill burden, iron supplementation | Black stools (expected), GI side effects |
| Ferric citrate (Auryxia) | 1-2 tablets with each meal | Dual benefit: phosphorus binding + iron supplementation | GI side effects, iron overload risk |

**Step 3: Active Vitamin D (calcitriol) adjustment**
- If on calcitriol or active vitamin D analog and phosphorus elevated: reduce or hold dose
- Phosphorus must be controlled before increasing active vitamin D (risk of calcium-phosphorus product elevation and vascular calcification)

### Target Calcium-Phosphorus Product
- Ca x Phos < 55 mg2/dL2 (KDOQI guideline)
- Elevated product associated with vascular calcification and cardiovascular mortality

## Calcium Management

### Targets

| CKD Stage | Calcium Target | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| All stages | 8.5-10.5 mg/dL (corrected for albumin) | |
| Dialysis | 8.4-9.5 mg/dL | Lower target to avoid hypercalcemia from binders/vitamin D |

### Correction for Albumin
Corrected Calcium = Total Calcium + 0.8 * (4.0 - Albumin)

### Hypocalcemia in CKD
Usually from:
- Hyperphosphatemia (calcium-phosphorus binding)
- Vitamin D deficiency (reduced 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D production by diseased kidneys)
- Elevated PTH (secondary hyperparathyroidism)

Management:
1. Correct phosphorus first (calcium supplements ineffective if phosphorus elevated)
2. Vitamin D repletion: ergocalciferol or cholecalciferol if 25-OH vitamin D < 30 ng/mL
3. Active vitamin D (calcitriol 0.25-0.5 mcg daily) if: PTH elevated despite adequate 25-OH vitamin D, or eGFR < 15
4. Calcium supplementation (calcium carbonate or citrate) if needed, but avoid > 1500mg/day elemental calcium

### Hypercalcemia in CKD
Usually from:
- Excessive calcium-based phosphate binders
- Excessive active vitamin D (calcitriol, paricalcitol)
- Tertiary hyperparathyroidism (autonomously elevated PTH after prolonged secondary)
- Adynamic bone disease (low bone turnover)

Management: reduce/stop calcium-based binders, reduce active vitamin D, switch to non-calcium phosphate binder

## Bicarbonate / Metabolic Acidosis Management

### Targets

| CKD Stage | Bicarbonate Target | Monitoring |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------|
| G1-G3a | 22-29 mEq/L | Annually |
| G3b-G4 | >= 22 mEq/L | Every 3-6 months |
| G5 / Dialysis | 22-26 mEq/L | Monthly (pre-dialysis) |

### Metabolic Acidosis Treatment

Rationale: Correction of metabolic acidosis in CKD slows GFR decline, reduces muscle wasting, and improves bone mineral density.

**Bicarbonate < 22 mEq/L in CKD G3-G5 (non-dialysis):**
1. Oral sodium bicarbonate: start 650mg (8 mEq) TID with meals
2. Titrate to maintain bicarbonate >= 22 mEq/L
3. Max dose: typically 1950-3900mg/day (24-48 mEq/day)
4. Monitor for sodium/fluid retention (each 650mg tablet = 8 mEq sodium)
5. Alternative: sodium citrate (Bicitra) 15-30mL daily (each 30mL = ~30 mEq bicarbonate equivalent)
6. Dietary: increase fruits and vegetables (base-producing); reduce animal protein (acid-producing)

**Bicarbonate < 15 mEq/L:**
- Assess urgency: check pH (if < 7.20, see critical value management)
- IV sodium bicarbonate if pH < 7.10 or hemodynamic instability
- Consider dialysis if refractory

## PTH and Mineral Bone Disease (CKD-MBD)

### PTH Targets by CKD Stage

| CKD Stage | PTH Target | Monitoring Interval |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| G3a-G3b | Within normal lab range (10-65 pg/mL typical) | Every 6-12 months |
| G4 | Within normal lab range | Every 6-12 months |
| G5 / Dialysis | 2-9x upper limit of normal (KDIGO) | Every 3-6 months (G5ND); monthly to every 3 months (dialysis) |

### Secondary Hyperparathyroidism Management Sequence

1. **Correct Vitamin D deficiency**: 25-OH Vitamin D < 30 ng/mL -> ergocalciferol 50,000 IU weekly x 8-12 weeks, then 1000-2000 IU daily maintenance
2. **Control phosphorus**: dietary restriction + binders (see above)
3. **Active Vitamin D analogs** (if PTH remains elevated despite adequate 25-OH D and phosphorus control):
   - Calcitriol 0.25-0.5 mcg daily or 0.5-1.0 mcg thrice weekly
   - Paricalcitol (Zemplar) 1-4 mcg thrice weekly (less hypercalcemia/hyperphosphatemia than calcitriol)
   - Doxercalciferol 2.5-5 mcg thrice weekly
4. **Calcimimetic** (if hypercalcemia limits vitamin D use, or refractory secondary hyperparathyroidism):
   - Cinacalcet (Sensipar) 30mg daily, titrate every 2-4 weeks (max 180mg). GI side effects common. Check calcium 1 week after dose change.
   - Etelcalcetide (Parsabiv) 5mg IV thrice weekly at end of dialysis (dialysis patients only)
5. **Parathyroidectomy**: for refractory hyperparathyroidism with PTH persistently > 800 pg/mL, hypercalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, calciphylaxis, or severe symptoms

### Vitamin D Monitoring

| CKD Stage | 25-OH Vitamin D Check | Target | Supplement |
|-----------|-----------------------|--------|-----------|
| G1-G3a | Annually | >= 30 ng/mL | Cholecalciferol 1000-2000 IU daily or ergocalciferol 50K IU weekly if deficient |
| G3b-G5 | Every 6-12 months | >= 30 ng/mL | Same; also consider active vitamin D if PTH elevated |
| Dialysis | Every 3-6 months | >= 30 ng/mL | Same; active vitamin D routinely needed |

## Dialysis Initiation Criteria

### Absolute Indications (Start Dialysis Regardless of eGFR)

| Indication | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Refractory hyperkalemia | K+ > 6.5 mEq/L despite maximal medical management (binders, insulin/dextrose, bicarbonate, diuretics) |
| Refractory fluid overload | Pulmonary edema or anasarca not responding to IV diuretics (furosemide 200-400mg IV or bumetanide 8-10mg IV) |
| Uremic pericarditis | Pericardial friction rub, chest pain, pericardial effusion in setting of uremia. Medical emergency -- do not delay dialysis. |
| Uremic encephalopathy | Altered mental status, asterixis, myoclonus, seizures attributable to uremia |
| Uremic bleeding | Qualitative platelet dysfunction causing significant bleeding (GI, mucocutaneous) |
| Severe metabolic acidosis | pH < 7.1 refractory to IV bicarbonate |
| Certain toxic ingestions | Methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, salicylate (when levels or clinical status warrant) |

### Relative Indications

| Indication | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| eGFR 5-10 with symptoms | Uremic symptoms: intractable nausea/vomiting, anorexia, fatigue, pruritus, restless legs, cognitive decline |
| Progressive malnutrition | Weight loss, decreasing albumin, loss of appetite despite dietary counseling |
| Neuropathy progression | Worsening peripheral neuropathy attributable to uremia |
| Refractory hypertension | Blood pressure not controlled despite 4+ antihypertensives with volume component |

### Timing Considerations
- IDEAL trial: starting dialysis at eGFR 10-14 vs 5-7 (symptom-guided) showed NO survival benefit for early start
- Current consensus: initiate dialysis based on symptoms, not a fixed eGFR threshold
- Prepare for dialysis when eGFR < 20: modality education, access planning
- Create AV fistula 6+ months before anticipated need (maturation time 2-6 months)
- Peritoneal dialysis catheter: place 2+ weeks before use

## Anemia of CKD

### Monitoring

| CKD Stage | Hemoglobin Check | Ferritin + TSAT Check |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| G3a-G3b | Every 6-12 months | If Hgb < 12 g/dL |
| G4-G5 (non-dialysis) | Every 3-6 months | Every 3-6 months |
| G5D (dialysis) | Monthly | Every 3 months |

### Iron Targets

| Parameter | Target (Non-Dialysis CKD) | Target (Dialysis) |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Ferritin | >= 100 ng/mL | >= 200 ng/mL (some guidelines suggest >= 500 as upper limit for dosing) |
| TSAT | >= 20% | >= 20% (some guidelines use >= 30%) |

### Iron Replacement

- Oral: ferrous sulfate 325mg (65mg elemental) daily on empty stomach. Limited absorption in CKD. Trial for 1-3 months.
- IV (if oral fails or dialysis patients): iron sucrose 200mg per dialysis session x 5 (total 1000mg); or ferric carboxymaltose 750mg x 2 (2 weeks apart); or ferumoxytol 510mg x 2 (1 week apart)

### Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs)

| Criteria to Start ESA | Hemoglobin |
|-----------------------|------------|
| Start ESA | Hgb < 10 g/dL (after iron replete and other causes of anemia excluded) |
| Target Hgb | 10-11.5 g/dL (KDIGO). Do NOT target > 13 g/dL (increased cardiovascular risk per CHOIR/CREATE/TREAT trials) |

Agents:
- Epoetin alfa (Epogen/Procrit): 50-100 U/kg IV/SC thrice weekly (dialysis); 50-100 U/kg SC weekly (non-dialysis)
- Darbepoetin alfa (Aranesp): 0.45 mcg/kg IV/SC every 1-2 weeks
- Methoxy polyethylene glycol-epoetin beta (Mircera): 0.6 mcg/kg IV/SC every 2 weeks

### HIF-PHI (newer oral agents)
- Roxadustat, daprodustat, vadadustat: oral agents that stabilize HIF (hypoxia-inducible factor) to stimulate erythropoiesis
- Approved in some markets for CKD anemia (dialysis and non-dialysis)
- Advantage: oral dosing, may improve iron absorption
- Caution: thromboembolic risk, long-term safety data still accruing

# CDC 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Prescribing Opioids

Source: CDC Clinical Practice Guideline for Prescribing Opioids for Pain - United States, 2022. MMWR Recommendations and Reports, 71(3):1-95.

## Guideline Scope

Applies to outpatient adults (>=18 years) with:
- Acute pain (duration <1 month)
- Subacute pain (duration 1-3 months)
- Chronic pain (duration >3 months)

Excludes:
- Cancer-related pain treatment
- Palliative care
- End-of-life care
- Sickle cell disease-related pain management

---

## 12 Recommendations Summary

### Determining Whether to Initiate Opioids

**Recommendation 1**: Nonopioid therapies are at least as effective as opioids for many common types of acute pain. Clinicians should maximize use of nonopioid pharmacologic and nonpharmacologic therapies as appropriate for the specific condition and patient and only consider opioid therapy for acute pain if benefits are anticipated to outweigh risks.

**Recommendation 2**: Nonopioid therapies are preferred for subacute and chronic pain. Clinicians should maximize use of nonopioid therapies and only consider initiating opioid therapy if expected benefits for pain and function are anticipated to outweigh risks.

### Opioid Selection, Dosage, Duration, Follow-Up, and Discontinuation

**Recommendation 3**: When starting opioid therapy, prescribe immediate-release opioids instead of extended-release/long-acting (ER/LA) opioids.

**Recommendation 4**: When opioids are started for opioid-naive patients, prescribe the lowest effective dosage. If opioids are continued, carefully reassess evidence of individual benefits and risks when considering increasing dosage. Avoid increasing dosage above levels likely to yield diminishing returns in benefits relative to risks. Before increasing total opioid dosage to >= 50 MME/day, carefully reassess. Avoid increasing to >= 90 MME/day, or carefully justify.

**Recommendation 5**: For acute pain, prescribe no greater quantity than needed for the expected duration. 3 days or less is often sufficient; more than 7 days is rarely needed.

**Recommendation 6**: When opioids are needed for subacute or chronic pain, evaluate benefits and risks within 1-4 weeks of starting. Ongoing assessment at least every 3 months. If benefits do not outweigh risks, work with patients to optimize nonopioid therapies, taper, and discontinue.

### Assessing Risk and Addressing Harms

**Recommendation 7**: Evaluate risk factors for opioid-related harms before and during therapy. Incorporate strategies to mitigate risk, including offering naloxone.

**Recommendation 8**: Before starting and periodically during opioid therapy, review the patient's history of controlled substance prescriptions using PDMP data.

**Recommendation 9**: When prescribing initial opioid therapy or considering dose escalation, use toxicology testing to assess for prescribed and non-prescribed substances.

**Recommendation 10**: Use particular caution when prescribing opioids with benzodiazepines, and consider whether benefits outweigh risks of concurrent prescribing.

**Recommendation 11**: Offer or arrange treatment with evidence-based medications (buprenorphine or methadone) for patients with opioid use disorder, with behavioral therapies.

**Recommendation 12**: Collaborate with patients to determine how to reduce risk when tapering opioids, continue to offer nonopioid pain management, and develop a plan to treat symptoms of OUD or opioid withdrawal.

## Key Thresholds and Triggers

### MME Thresholds

| Threshold | Significance | Action |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| < 20 MME/day | Low risk zone | Standard monitoring |
| 20-49 MME/day | Moderate caution | Reassess risk/benefit at each visit |
| >= 50 MME/day | Elevated risk | Carefully reassess evidence of benefits vs risks. Prescribe naloxone. |
| >= 90 MME/day | Substantial risk | Avoid increasing to this level. If already at this level, carefully justify or taper. Prescribe naloxone. |

### Overdose Risk Multipliers

Based on epidemiologic data:
| MME/day | Overdose Risk vs No Opioid |
|---------|---------------------------|
| 1-19 | 1x baseline |
| 20-49 | ~2x |
| 50-89 | ~3-4x |
| 90-119 | ~5-7x |
| >= 120 | ~9x+ |

### Duration Thresholds for Acute Pain

| Condition | Recommended Duration | Max Without Reassessment |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| Dental procedures | 0-3 days | 3 days |
| Minor surgery | 0-3 days | 7 days |
| Musculoskeletal injury | 0-3 days | 7 days |
| Low back pain (acute) | Avoid opioids if possible | 7 days |
| Fractures | 3-7 days | 14 days |
| Major surgery | 3-7 days | 14 days |
| Kidney stones | 0-3 days | 5 days |

## Naloxone Co-Prescribing Criteria

### When to Prescribe Naloxone

Prescribe naloxone when ANY of the following apply:

1. Total daily opioid dosage >= 50 MME
2. Concurrent benzodiazepine prescription
3. Concurrent gabapentinoid prescription (gabapentin, pregabalin)
4. History of overdose
5. History of substance use disorder
6. Higher opioid dosages (>= 50 MME/day)
7. Patient request
8. Patients at risk for returning to a high dose after losing tolerance (e.g., recently released from incarceration, recently completed OUD treatment, recently tapered from long-term therapy)

### Patient Education Points for Naloxone

1. Keep naloxone accessible (not locked away)
2. Educate household members and close contacts on use
3. Signs of overdose: pinpoint pupils, unresponsive, slow/no breathing, blue lips/fingertips
4. Call 911 immediately even if naloxone is administered
5. Naloxone may need to be repeated (especially for long-acting opioids or fentanyl)
6. Place patient in recovery position (on side) after administration
7. Stay with the person until emergency services arrive
8. Naloxone will wear off before the opioid - overdose can return

## Tapering Protocols

### When to Consider Tapering

1. Patient is not meeting treatment goals (pain relief, functional improvement)
2. Patient is on high-dose opioids (>= 90 MME) without evidence of continued benefit
3. Patient experiences significant side effects
4. Patient demonstrates aberrant behaviors
5. Patient has a new condition increasing risk (respiratory disease, fall risk)
6. Patient requests tapering

### General Tapering Principles

- **Rate**: Reduce by 5-10% of the original dose per month. Slower tapers (5% every 2-4 weeks) are better tolerated.
- **Pause if needed**: If withdrawal symptoms are significant, hold at current dose for 1-2 months before resuming taper.
- **Slower at lower doses**: As total dose decreases, the percentage reduction represents a smaller absolute amount. Consider slowing to 2-5% per month when below 30% of original dose.
- **Avoid abrupt discontinuation**: Abrupt cessation causes severe withdrawal and may lead to illicit opioid use.
- **Minimum taper duration**: Patients on long-term opioids should be tapered over months, not weeks.
- **Monitor for withdrawal**: Assess at each taper step.
- **Monitor for undertreated pain**: Optimize nonopioid therapies during taper.
- **Monitor for behavioral health changes**: Depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation can emerge during taper.

### Withdrawal Symptoms to Monitor

| Early (6-24 hours) | Late (1-3 days) | Protracted (weeks-months) |
|--------------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Anxiety, restlessness | Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea | Dysphoria, anxiety |
| Yawning, tearing | Muscle aches, cramping | Insomnia |
| Rhinorrhea | Piloerection ("goosebumps") | Fatigue |
| Sweating | Insomnia | Drug cravings |
| Muscle twitching | Tachycardia, hypertension | Difficulty concentrating |
| Mydriasis (dilated pupils) | Fever | Irritability |

### Supportive Medications During Taper

| Medication | Purpose | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Clonidine 0.1-0.2mg BID-TID | Autonomic symptoms (sweating, tachycardia, anxiety) | Monitor for hypotension |
| Loperamide PRN | Diarrhea | Standard OTC dosing |
| Ondansetron PRN | Nausea/vomiting | 4-8mg every 8 hours |
| Ibuprofen/Acetaminophen | Muscle aches | Standard dosing |
| Hydroxyzine 25-50mg PRN | Anxiety, insomnia | Non-addictive anxiolytic |
| Trazodone 25-50mg QHS | Insomnia | Low-dose for sleep |

## Monitoring Schedule

### Before Starting Opioids
- [ ] Complete pain assessment with functional goals
- [ ] Review PDMP
- [ ] Obtain urine drug screen
- [ ] Calculate ORT score
- [ ] Administer CAGE-AID
- [ ] Review medical history for risk factors
- [ ] Document informed consent discussion
- [ ] Start with immediate-release formulation
- [ ] Prescribe naloxone if any risk factors present

### During Opioid Therapy

| Monitoring Activity | Frequency |
|-------------------|-----------|
| In-person reassessment | 1-4 weeks after initiation, then every 1-3 months |
| Pain and function assessment | Every visit |
| PDMP check | Before each prescription (at minimum every 3 months) |
| Urine drug screen | At least annually; every 1-3 months if moderate/high risk |
| MME calculation | At each dose change and at least annually |
| Naloxone check | At each visit |
| Risk factor reassessment | At least annually |
| Taper discussion | When benefits no longer outweigh risks |

### Red Flags Requiring Immediate Reassessment
1. Unexpected positive UDS (non-prescribed substances)
2. Unexpected negative UDS (prescribed opioid absent - possible diversion)
3. PDMP showing prescriptions from multiple providers
4. Requesting early refills (>1 occurrence)
5. Reporting stolen or lost prescriptions (>1 occurrence)
6. Evidence of intoxication at visit
7. Overdose event
8. New substance use disorder diagnosis
9. Functional decline despite stable or increasing doses
10. Patient reports using opioids for non-pain purposes

# Adherence Interventions Reference

## Motivational Interviewing (MI) for Medication Adherence

### Core MI Principles (OARS)

**Open-ended Questions**: Explore the patient's perspective without judgment.
- "What has your experience been with this medication?"
- "How do you feel about taking medications for your [condition]?"
- "What concerns do you have about your medications?"
- "Tell me about a typical day and how your medications fit in."
- "What would need to change for you to feel comfortable taking this medication regularly?"

**Affirmations**: Acknowledge the patient's efforts and strengths.
- "It sounds like you've been really thoughtful about your health."
- "Coming in today shows you care about managing your [condition]."
- "You've done well keeping up with [X medication] - that takes commitment."

**Reflective Listening**: Mirror the patient's statements to demonstrate understanding.
- Patient: "I just forget sometimes." -> "Forgetting is a common challenge, especially with busy schedules."
- Patient: "I don't think the medicine helps." -> "You're not sure the medication is making a difference for you."
- Patient: "I stopped because of the side effects." -> "The side effects were significant enough that you decided to stop."

**Summarizing**: Tie together what the patient has shared.
- "So it sounds like [medication] has been hard to keep up with because of [barrier], and you'd be more willing to take it if [condition]. Is that right?"

### MI Prompts by Barrier Type

#### Cost / Insurance Barriers
- "If cost is a barrier, let's look at what options might be available - generics, assistance programs, or alternative medications."
- "What would be a manageable amount for you to spend on medications each month?"

#### Side Effects
- "Which side effects have been most bothersome for you?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how much do the side effects affect your daily life?"
- "Would you be willing to try a different medication that might have fewer side effects?"

#### Perceived Lack of Need
- "What do you understand about what [condition] does to your body over time?"
- "How important is it to you to prevent [complication] in the future?"
- "What would convince you that this medication is worth taking?"

#### Forgetfulness
- "What reminders or routines have worked for you in the past for other habits?"
- "Would linking your medication to something you already do every day - like brushing your teeth - help?"

#### Complexity / Pill Burden
- "Taking [N] medications a day is a lot. Which ones feel the most important to you?"
- "Would it help to simplify your regimen if we could combine some medications?"

## Evidence-Based Adherence Intervention Strategies

### Tier 1: Low-Intensity Interventions (for PDC 60-80%)

| Intervention | Description | Evidence Level |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| Reminder systems | Phone alarms, pill organizers, blister packs | Moderate |
| Simplification | Switch to once-daily formulations, combination pills | Strong |
| Refill synchronization | Align all medications to same fill date | Moderate |
| 90-day supplies | Reduce pharmacy visits from 12 to 4 per year | Strong |
| Mail-order pharmacy | Automatic delivery, often lower copay | Moderate |
| Medication therapy management (MTM) | Pharmacist-led comprehensive review | Strong |

### Tier 2: Moderate-Intensity Interventions (for PDC 40-60%)

| Intervention | Description | Evidence Level |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| Motivational interviewing | Structured patient-centered counseling | Strong |
| Patient education | Teach-back method for medication purpose and use | Moderate |
| Shared decision-making | Involve patient in treatment choices | Strong |
| Address side effects | Proactive management or medication switches | Strong |
| Depression screening | PHQ-9; treat comorbid depression | Strong |
| Social support | Family/caregiver involvement in medication management | Moderate |
| Cost reduction | Generic substitution, manufacturer coupons, patient assistance programs | Strong |

### Tier 3: High-Intensity Interventions (for PDC <40%)

| Intervention | Description | Evidence Level |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| Directly observed therapy (DOT) | Supervised medication administration | Strong (TB, HIV) |
| Home health visits | Nursing visits to assist with medication management | Moderate |
| Long-acting formulations | Monthly injections instead of daily pills (e.g., paliperidone, cabotegravir) | Strong |
| Smart pill bottles | Electronic monitoring with feedback (MEMS caps) | Moderate |
| Intensive case management | Dedicated care coordinator for high-risk patients | Moderate |
| Community health workers | Culturally concordant support and navigation | Moderate |
| Behavioral contracts | Written agreement between patient and provider | Weak |

## Adherence Barrier Categories and Screening Questions

### Screening Tool: Adherence Barrier Assessment

Ask systematically to identify the root cause:

**1. Knowledge Barriers**
- "Can you tell me why you take [medication]?"
- "What do you think would happen if you stopped taking it?"
- Red flag: Patient cannot name the medication or its purpose.

**2. Recall / Cognitive Barriers**
- "How often do you forget to take your medications?"
- "Do you have a system for remembering?"
- "Has your memory gotten worse recently?"
- Red flag: Patient forgets doses multiple times per week.

**3. Motivation Barriers**
- "On a scale of 1-10, how important is taking this medication to you?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can take it every day?"
- Red flag: Importance or confidence score <5.

**4. Side Effect Barriers**
- "Have you experienced any side effects from your medications?"
- "Did you ever stop a medication because of how it made you feel?"
- Red flag: Patient reports stopping medication due to side effects without telling provider.

**5. Access Barriers**
- "Have you ever not filled a prescription because of cost?"
- "Is it easy for you to get to the pharmacy?"
- "Do you ever run out of medications before you can get a refill?"
- Red flag: Patient reports cost-related non-adherence.

**6. Belief Barriers**
- "Do you have any concerns about taking medications long-term?"
- "Do you prefer to use natural remedies instead?"
- "Do you feel your condition is serious enough to need medication?"
- Red flag: Patient expresses strong negative medication beliefs.

**7. System Barriers**
- "Have you had trouble getting authorization for any medications?"
- "Have you had a medication change that was confusing?"
- "Do you see multiple doctors who prescribe medications?"
- Red flag: Patient describes fragmented care or authorization difficulties.

## Intervention Selection Algorithm

```
IF PDC >= 80%:
  -> Reinforce. "You're doing a great job. Keep it up."
  -> Monitor at regular intervals.

IF PDC 60-79%:
  -> Screen for barriers (Section above)
  -> IF forgetfulness: Tier 1 (reminders, simplification, 90-day supply)
  -> IF complexity: Tier 1 (simplification, combination products)
  -> IF cost: Tier 2 (generic switch, assistance programs)
  -> Reassess in 3 months

IF PDC 40-59%:
  -> Screen for barriers
  -> IF side effects: Tier 2 (medication change, dose adjustment)
  -> IF motivation/beliefs: Tier 2 (motivational interviewing)
  -> IF depression: Tier 2 (PHQ-9 screening, treatment)
  -> IF multiple barriers: Tier 2 (MTM, shared decision-making)
  -> Reassess in 1 month

IF PDC < 40%:
  -> Screen for barriers
  -> IF all barriers: Tier 3 (intensive case management)
  -> IF access: Tier 3 (home health, mail-order)
  -> IF cognitive: Tier 3 (DOT, smart bottles, caregiver involvement)
  -> IF intentional non-adherence: Tier 2-3 (MI + consider discontinuation if appropriate)
  -> Reassess in 2 weeks
```

## Measuring Intervention Effectiveness

### Before/After Comparison

After implementing an adherence intervention, reassess PDC at:
- 1 month (early signal)
- 3 months (short-term effectiveness)
- 6 months (sustained effect)
- 12 months (long-term maintenance)

### Success Criteria
- PDC improvement of >= 10 percentage points
- PDC crossing the 80% threshold
- Improvement in clinical outcome (HbA1c, BP, LDL)
- Patient-reported improvement in medication experience

### Documentation in FHIR

Use MedicationStatement.adherence extension or a clinical note (Composition) to document:
- Adherence assessment date
- PDC values calculated
- Barriers identified
- Intervention implemented
- Follow-up plan

# CDC 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Prescribing Opioids Reference

## Key Recommendations Summary

### Recommendation 1: Non-Opioid Therapy First
- Non-pharmacologic therapy (exercise, cognitive behavioral therapy, multimodal approaches) and non-opioid pharmacologic therapy are PREFERRED for chronic pain
- Opioids should NOT be the first-line treatment for chronic pain

### Recommendation 2: Treatment Goals
- Before starting opioids, establish realistic treatment goals focused on FUNCTION, not just pain scores
- Use validated tools: PEG scale, Oswestry Disability Index, PROMIS measures
- Continue opioid therapy only if there is clinically meaningful improvement in BOTH pain AND function

### Recommendation 3: Initial Opioid Selection
- If opioids are indicated: immediate-release opioids are preferred over extended-release/long-acting
- Start at lowest effective dose
- Prescribe no greater quantity than needed for expected duration of severe pain

### Recommendation 4: Follow-Up and Reassessment
| Timepoint | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| 1-4 weeks after starting | Evaluate benefits and harms |
| Every 3 months (minimum) | Reassess continued need |
| At each visit | Review pain scores, function, adverse effects |
| Before dose increase | Verify functional benefit at current dose |

### Recommendation 5: Risk Assessment
- Before and periodically during opioid therapy:
  - Check state PDMP (every prescription or at least every 3 months)
  - Assess for opioid use disorder using validated tools
  - Obtain urine drug testing (baseline and at least annually; more frequently for higher risk)

### Recommendation 6: MME Awareness
- Use caution at any dosage
- Carefully reassess benefits vs risks when increasing to >= 50 MME/day
- Avoid increasing to >= 90 MME/day or carefully justify continued use

### Recommendation 7: Concurrent Prescribing
- Avoid concurrent opioid and benzodiazepine prescribing (FDA Boxed Warning)
- If both clinically necessary, document justification and closely monitor

## Non-Opioid Pain Management Options

### Pharmacologic

| Class | Examples | Best For | Key Notes |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Acetaminophen | Acetaminophen | Mild-moderate pain, OA | Max 3g/day; avoid in liver disease |
| NSAIDs (oral) | Ibuprofen, naproxen, celecoxib | Inflammatory pain, OA, MSK | GI/renal/CV risk with chronic use |
| Topical NSAIDs | Diclofenac gel/patch | Localized OA, MSK pain | Lower systemic risk |
| Topical lidocaine | Lidocaine 5% patch | Neuropathic, localized pain | Apply to painful area, 12h on/12h off |
| Topical capsaicin | Capsaicin 0.025-0.075% cream | Neuropathic, OA | Initial burning; takes 2-4 weeks |
| Duloxetine (SNRI) | Duloxetine 30-60mg | Neuropathic, fibromyalgia, chronic MSK | FDA-approved for multiple pain conditions |
| Gabapentin | 300-3600mg/day | Neuropathic pain | Titrate slowly; sedation, dizziness |
| Pregabalin | 150-600mg/day | Neuropathic, fibromyalgia | FDA-approved for fibromyalgia |
| TCAs | Amitriptyline 10-75mg, nortriptyline | Neuropathic pain, headache | Anticholinergic effects; use lower doses in elderly |
| Muscle relaxants | Cyclobenzaprine, tizanidine | Acute MSK spasm (short-term) | Sedation; avoid chronic use |

## PEG Scale (Pain, Enjoyment, General Activity)

Three questions, each rated 0-10:
1. What number best describes your pain on average in the past week? (0 = no pain, 10 = worst imaginable)
2. What number describes how, during the past week, pain has interfered with your enjoyment of life? (0 = does not interfere, 10 = completely interferes)
3. What number describes how, during the past week, pain has interfered with your general activity? (0 = does not interfere, 10 = completely interferes)

**PEG Score = Average of 3 items**
- Meaningful improvement: >= 30% reduction in PEG score from baseline
- Track over time to assess treatment response

## Opioid Tapering Guidance

### When to Taper
- Opioids not meeting treatment goals (no functional improvement)
- Side effects outweigh benefits
- Dose exceeds 90 MME/day without clear benefit
- Concurrent high-risk medications
- Signs of opioid use disorder
- Patient request

### Tapering Rate
| Situation | Rate |
|-----------|------|
| Voluntary / planned | 10% per month (slow, well-tolerated) |
| Moderate urgency | 10% per week |
| Acute safety concern | Faster taper with close monitoring (not abrupt cessation) |

**Never abruptly discontinue opioids** in patients on chronic therapy -- withdrawal risk.

### Monitoring During Taper
- Pain scores and functional measures every visit
- Withdrawal symptoms (COWS scale for objective withdrawal)
- Mental health assessment (depression, anxiety may worsen)
- Substance use monitoring (risk of illicit opioid use increases during taper)
